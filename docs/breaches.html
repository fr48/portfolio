Breaking Records: More Data Breaches

If you are tired of data breach notifications in the mail and news, there's some bad news: More people will be impacted in 2019 than in 2018.

Fahmida Y Rashid

Published July 16, 2019

2019 is shaping up to be a rough year when it comes to their information being stolen or exposed online.

Practically every person in the United States has been impacted by at least one security incident or data breach. Large retailers, government agencies, universities, and banks: no one seems to be immune.

Breaches Rising

Each year, there are more data breaches. 2018 was a particularly bad year, but the first half of 2019 already has more breaches than the first half of 2018, and the pace of breaches hasn't slowed down.

Source: Identity Theft Resource Center

Any company or organization can get breached, because every organization has information that is valuable to someone. While breaches at businesses, such as retailers, hotels, and restaurants have dominated the new over the past five years, there have been a number of bad healthcare-related breaches in recent years.

Who is Losing the Data?

While anyone can be breached, businesses and healthcare organizations are the most affected. While government, education, and financial services have data breaches, they are fewer in number.

Source: Identity Theft Resource Center

What Has Been Stolen

Different states have different rules about breach notifications. Massachusettes is among the few states that require organizations to be explicit about what types of information have been compromised. Social Security numbers are by far the most common pieces of data exposed in 2018 and 2019. More SSN were stolen in the first half of 2019 than the first half of 2018.

Account numbers -- not just bank accounts, but rewards programs, frequent flier miles, and billing information at other ecommerce sites -- are also higly sought for. There were more breaches that exposed payment cards -- credit and debit cards -- than in previous years. Interestingly, more drivers license numbers were stolen in the first half of 2019 than all of 2018.

Stolen Records

Account numbers and Social Security numbers are the most commonly targeted pieces of information, even at healthcare organizations.

Sources: Office of Consumer Affairs and Business Regulation, Commonwealth of Massachusetts

Breach Fatigue Sets In

Every week, there is a new report about a data breach. Sometimes, it's cybercriminals breaking into organizations to steal data and other times it's nation-state spies intent on collecting information for their espionage games. Many times, it's a simple matter of the company losing the data (say, a laptop holding a spreadsheet) rather than someone nefarious thief. There are also times when the organization forgot to protect the information and left it on the Internet for any curious person to find. Then there are times when we simply don't know what happened. But whatever the reason, most American adults have been affected at least once by a data breach over the past few years. And for many, they have become resigned and no longer may be as concerned with a breach.
